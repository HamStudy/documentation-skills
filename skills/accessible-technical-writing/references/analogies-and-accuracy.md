# Analogies and Accuracy

How to use analogies effectively and verify factual claims. Read this when writing or
reviewing analogies, checking suspect facts, or when content includes anecdotes,

## Analogies that illuminate

### Why analogies matter

Analogies are the most powerful tool in educational writing and the most dangerous.
When one works — telomeres described as "the plastic caps at the tips of shoelaces" —
it creates an instant mental model anchoring an abstract concept in physical experience.
When one fails — "the brain is a computer" implying binary logic and rewritable
memory — it installs a misconception harder to correct than the original ignorance.

### The mapping-accuracy test

Every analogy has a **vehicle** (the familiar thing) and a **tenor** (the thing being
explained). The quality of the analogy depends on the accuracy of the mapping **in the
dimension being explained**.

Every analogy breaks down somewhere. The question is whether it breaks down in the
specific area you're trying to illuminate.

**Good analogy**: "Electric current is like water flowing through a pipe" — when
explaining the relationship between pressure (voltage), flow rate (current), and pipe
diameter (resistance). The mapping holds in the dimension being taught.

**Bad analogy**: "Data is the new oil" — when discussing data economics. Oil is
non-renewable and loses value after use; data is the opposite. The mapping breaks in
the exact dimension under discussion.

### Rules for analogy use

1. **Use analogies as entry points, not replacements.** The analogy opens the door;
   the actual explanation follows. Never leave an analogy as the sole explanation.

2. **One analogy per concept.** Choose one well-matched analogy and commit to it.
   Multiple analogies for the same thing create confusion, not clarity.

3. **State where the analogy breaks down.** "Unlike water in pipes, electricity
   doesn't physically 'flow' in the same sense — but this model captures the
   relationship between pressure and flow rate." This builds trust and prevents
   over-extension.

4. **Spell out the mapping.** Every analogy must have a clear, articulable mapping —
   what corresponds to what. If you can't state the mapping explicitly, the analogy
   is decorative, not explanatory.

5. **Prefer everyday physical experience** over pop-culture references. Pop-culture
   dates quickly and excludes audiences unfamiliar with the reference. Physical
   experience is nearly universal.

6. **Never reuse the same analogy template.** "X is the Swiss Army knife of Y"
   appearing once is fine. Appearing twice is noticeable. Appearing thirty times is
   a crisis. Each analogy should be fresh and specific to its concept.

7. **Test for misleading implications.** Ask: "If the reader took this analogy
   literally and extended it, would they reach a false conclusion?" If yes, either
   add an explicit boundary statement or choose a different analogy.

### When NOT to use analogies

- When the concept is simple enough to explain directly. Analogies for simple things
  feel patronizing.
- When no good mapping exists. A forced analogy is worse than no analogy.
- When the concept is so far from everyday experience that any analogy would be
  misleading. Sometimes direct, careful explanation is the only honest approach.

## The illustration vs. assertion distinction

This distinction dramatically reduces factual risk in educational writing.

**Illustrations** are explicitly hypothetical. They use framing like:
- "Imagine..."
- "Think of it like..."
- "Suppose you..."
- "Picture a scenario where..."

Illustrations have **low factual risk** — their job is correct mapping to the concept,
not historical accuracy. They don't need sourcing.

**Assertions** are presented as real. They use framing like:
- "In 19XX, [person] did..."
- "Scientists found that..."
- "Research shows..."
- "[Group] often..."
- "The word comes from..."
- "[Statistic]% of..."

Assertions have **high factual risk**. Every assertion requires a source — or it must
be reframed as an illustration. If you can't verify an anecdote, rewrite it as a
hypothetical scenario that makes the same pedagogical point.

**The conversion rule:** If an assertion can't be sourced, convert it to an
illustration. "In 1923, a ship ran aground because..." (assertion, needs source)
becomes "Imagine a ship approaching a harbor at night..." (illustration, no source
needed, same pedagogical value).

## Verification: the SIFT method

Mike Caulfield's SIFT approach provides a lightweight, high-leverage method for
verifying content that "sounds right" but might not be:

1. **Stop** — Pause before including any claim. Don't let plausibility substitute
   for verification.

2. **Investigate the source** — Who originally made this claim? Are they credible
   in this domain? Is the source primary or is it a retelling of a retelling?

3. **Find better coverage** — Look for authoritative sources on the same claim.
   Government agencies, peer-reviewed research, established institutions, and
   domain-specific authorities are preferred over secondary sources, blogs, and
   aggregators.

4. **Trace to original context** — Follow quotes and statistics upstream to their
   original source. Check that the claim means what it appears to mean in context.
   Many widely-cited statistics are taken out of context or misconstrued.

### When to apply SIFT

Apply SIFT to every sentence labeled [D] (requires external source) in the accuracy
pass. Also apply it to:

- Any anecdote presented as a real event
- Any statistic or numerical claim
- Any "origin story" for a term, technology, or practice
- Any attributed quote
- Any claim that sounds surprisingly neat or dramatic (these are the most likely
  to be embellished or fabricated in retellings)

## Anti-fabrication rules

These apply whenever AI is involved in generating or editing content:

1. **Never fabricate** facts, statistics, quotes, historical anecdotes, etymologies,
   or attributions. If you're not confident a fact is accurate, don't include it.

2. **Never invent idioms or phrases.** If uncertain whether a phrase is a real,
   widely-recognized idiom, use literal language instead. Made-up idioms are
   immediately noticeable and undermine credibility.

3. **Prefer general references over specific citations** when confidence is moderate.
   "Research suggests..." is honest when you know the general finding but not the
   specific study. "A 2019 study at MIT found..." is only acceptable when you can
   verify the study exists.

4. **Flag uncertainty explicitly** during drafting. Mark claims you're not sure about
   with [VERIFY] so the accuracy pass catches them. It's better to flag ten things
   that turn out to be fine than to miss one fabrication.

5. **Never present a plausible-sounding story as fact.** AI can generate anecdotes
   that have the right cadence and structure but didn't actually happen. Every
   narrative presented as real must be verified.

## Sourcing standards

When including factual claims, prefer sources in this order:

1. **Primary sources** — The original research paper, government regulation, official
   specification, or firsthand account.
2. **Authoritative secondary sources** — Well-established reference works, textbooks
   by recognized experts, official documentation.
3. **Reputable journalism** — Major outlets with editorial standards and correction
   policies.
4. **Domain-specific community sources** — When the topic is practical/applied and
   the community source has demonstrated expertise.

Avoid:
- Unsourced blog posts and forum comments (unless the author is a recognized expert)
- "According to some sources..." (vague attribution is worse than no attribution)
- Statistics without context (sample size, methodology, date, population)
- Secondhand retellings of anecdotes (trace to the original)
