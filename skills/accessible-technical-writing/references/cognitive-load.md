# Cognitive Load Management

How human memory constrains what readers can absorb, and how to design around those
constraints. Read this when content feels overwhelming, disorganized, or when readers
report getting lost.

## The working memory bottleneck

Human working memory can actively process roughly **four items simultaneously**
(Cowan, 2001 — revised downward from Miller's classic "seven plus or minus two").
Every new interacting element in your text competes for those four slots.

This is the single most important constraint in educational writing. If a paragraph
asks the reader to hold five unfamiliar concepts in mind simultaneously, comprehension
collapses regardless of how well each concept is individually explained.

## Three types of cognitive load

**Intrinsic load** is the inherent complexity of the material — how many pieces of
information must be processed simultaneously to understand a concept. You can't
eliminate it, but you can manage it through sequencing: present sub-components
separately before combining them.

**Extraneous load** is cognitive effort wasted on poor design — confusing structure,
irrelevant information, unclear language, redundant explanations, decorative-but-
distracting tangents. This is the primary target for reduction. Every sentence that
doesn't serve the learning objective contributes extraneous load.

**Germane load** is the productive kind — effort devoted to building understanding
and forming mental schemas. This is what you want to maximize.

The design goal of every writing decision: minimize extraneous load, manage intrinsic
load through sequencing, and maximize germane load by directing attention to the
right things.

## Seductive details

Richard Mayer's coherence principle identifies one of the most counterintuitive
sources of extraneous load: interesting but irrelevant tangents.

Fascinating historical asides, clever but tangential analogies, and entertaining but
off-topic stories are often remembered *better* than essential content — which means
they crowd essential content out of working memory. Even accurate, cool tangents can
become "seductive details" if they are entertaining but not structurally necessary to
the learning goal.

This is the research-backed version of "don't go on fun tangents that derail the
point" — even if the tangent is true and cool.

**Countermeasures:**
- Before including any anecdote, aside, or tangent, ask: "Does this directly support
  the outcome statement for this section?" If no, cut it or move it to an optional
  sidebar.
- When you can't bear to cut something interesting, put it in a clearly demarcated
  callout box where it won't interfere with the main learning path.
- Never let a tangent interrupt a multi-step explanation. Finish the explanation first;
  tangent later (if at all).

## Chunking rules

Chunking translates working memory limits into structural rules.

**Paragraph level:**
- Each paragraph introduces, explains, or illustrates **one main idea**.
- If a paragraph contains two ideas, split it.
- If you can't state the paragraph's single idea in one sentence, it's doing too much.

**Grouping level:**
- Any grouping of items (causes, features, steps, types) should contain **3–4 elements
  maximum**.
- When more exist, create meaningful sub-groups rather than presenting a wall of items.
  "There are three main categories, each with a few subtypes" is easier to process than
  "There are nine types."

**Section level:**
- Headers serve as **schema labels** — they help readers organize information into
  mental compartments. Use descriptive headers that tell the reader what they'll learn,
  not clever headers that require reading to decode.
- Between chunks, include a **bridging sentence** that explicitly links the previous
  idea to the next one. Don't rely on the reader to infer the connection.

**Sentence level:**
- Introduce no more than **one new term per sentence**.
- If a sentence requires the reader to hold more than two unfamiliar ideas in mind
  simultaneously, break it into multiple sentences that build incrementally.

## Scaffolding: Familiar → Bridge → New

The scaffolding principle governs sequencing within sections:

1. **Familiar** — Begin with something the reader already knows or can immediately
   relate to. This activates existing mental schemas and gives new information
   somewhere to attach.

2. **Bridge** — Use the familiar thing to connect to the unfamiliar concept. This is
   where analogies, comparisons, and "it's like X, except..." constructions live.

3. **New** — Develop the new idea, now that the reader has a framework for it.

Rules:
- Never use a term before defining it.
- Never reference a concept before introducing it.
- Always build from what the reader likely already knows to what they don't.
- For complex concepts, provide **multiple access routes**: a definition, an analogy,
  and a concrete example at minimum. Different readers will "get it" through different
  routes.

## The worked example effect

The worked example effect is described as "the best known and most widely studied of
the cognitive load effects." Novices learn more effectively by studying fully
worked-out examples than by being given principles and asked to apply them.

In educational writing (not just procedural content), this means:
- **Walk through applications step by step** rather than stating a concept and
  expecting readers to infer applications.
- **Show each intermediate step.** Never jump from problem to solution.
- **Explain why each step follows from the previous one.** The reasoning is as
  important as the steps themselves.
- **Use fading**: show a full example first, then a partially-worked example, then
  let the reader try independently. This provides structure early without spoon-feeding
  forever.

A good worked example answers three questions at each step:
1. What are we doing?
2. Why are we doing it?
3. What happens as a result?
