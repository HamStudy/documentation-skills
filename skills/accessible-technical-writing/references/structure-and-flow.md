# Structure and Narrative Flow

How to structure sections so they read like a journey, not a reference manual. Read
this before drafting any new content or when fixing weak openings, poor transitions,
or content that feels like disconnected topics.

## The structural insight

Ed Yong, Pulitzer Prize–winning science writer: "99 percent of writing problems are
actually structuring problems."

The difference between content that reads like a reference manual and content that
reads like a compelling narrative is almost entirely structural. If your content feels
flat, don't reach for better words first — fix the structure.

## Three opening frameworks

Different sections call for different openings. These three frameworks cover nearly
every situation. Pick one per section; don't mix them within a single opening.

### ABT (And–But–Therefore)

Randy Olson's narrative framework creates momentum, tension, and payoff.

- **AND**: "Here's what's generally true / what you already know..."
- **BUT**: "Here's the snag / common confusion / real constraint..."
- **THEREFORE**: "Here's what we're going to learn to resolve it..."

ABT adds narrative movement without requiring invented stories. It works especially
well for sections that address misconceptions or reveal hidden complexity.

**Example:**
> You can memorize the rules for which frequencies to use [AND], but when interference
> hits and you need to troubleshoot, memorization breaks down [BUT]. So we'll build a
> mental model of how radio waves actually behave — one you can apply to situations
> you've never seen before [THEREFORE].

### SCQA (Situation–Complication–Question–Answer)

Barbara Minto's Pyramid Principle approach. Aligns writer and reader on context before
delivering the answer.

- **Situation**: Where the reader is right now (what they already know/have)
- **Complication**: What goes wrong, what's missing, what's harder than expected
- **Question**: The implied question this section answers (can be implicit — you don't
  have to write "Why should we care...?")
- **Answer**: What this section gives the reader

SCQA works well for chapters that address a practical challenge or build on previous
material. The "Q" can be felt rather than stated — express it as a problem, not a
rhetorical question.

### BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)

State the main point early, then support it. From military and journalism traditions.

For book chapters, BLUF doesn't mean spoiling everything — it means making the point
of the section findable on the first page. Give the reader a framework for absorbing
everything that follows.

**Example:**
> Electronics show up everywhere in this hobby because they're how we turn energy into
> signals — and signals back into information. This chapter gives you a mental model
> for the three most important electrical relationships.

### Choosing a framework

- **ABT** when the section addresses a misconception, reveals hidden complexity, or
  builds tension between what seems true and what is true.
- **SCQA** when the section solves a practical problem or builds on prior knowledge.
- **BLUF** when the section introduces a new domain or foundational concept and the
  reader needs orientation.

Vary your choice across sections. If three consecutive sections all use ABT, the
pattern becomes visible and feels formulaic.

## Motivation without meta-commentary

"Start with why" means embed motivation naturally — not announce it. The reader should
feel the relevance of a topic before being told about it.

### Patterns that work

**Problem-first**: Open with a concrete problem that the upcoming concept resolves.
The reader experiences the knowledge gap directly.
> You type "google.com" into your browser and a webpage appears. But computers don't
> understand words — they only understand numbers. So how does your browser know where
> to go?

**Stakes-through-story**: Open with a narrative scene that implicitly demonstrates why
the concept matters — a real incident, a historical moment, a concrete scenario.

**"What breaks without it"**: Show consequences of not understanding the concept.
> In 1999, NASA lost a $125 million Mars orbiter because one team used metric units
> and another used imperial.

**Curiosity-first**: Reveal a surprising fact that implies there's more to understand.
> Every time you search Google, your query travels to a data center and back in under
> 400 milliseconds.

### Patterns that fail

- **"Why should we care about X?"** — Patronizing rhetorical question.
- **"You might be wondering why..."** — Presumptuous and formulaic.
- **"This is important because..."** — Announces motivation instead of creating it.
- **"In today's world..."** — Generic and content-free.

The common thread: **motivation is embedded in narrative, not in meta-commentary.**
The writer never says "This is important because..." Instead, they create a situation
where the importance is self-evident. The itch before the scratch.

### The anti-template rule

The reader should not be able to reverse-engineer your writing instructions from the
output. If your opening sounds like it was generated from a template, rewrite it until
it sounds like a human who is genuinely interested in the topic decided where to begin.

## Engagement architecture

Within each section, follow this pattern (flexibly, not rigidly):

1. **Hook** — A surprising fact, micro-narrative, counterintuitive statement, or
   question that creates an information gap. Keep it to 1–3 sentences.

2. **Context and motivation** — Why this matters, shown through story or example.
   Not stated directly ("This is important...") but demonstrated. Should flow
   naturally from the hook.

3. **Explanation** — The core content, built from concrete to abstract. Use the
   Familiar → Bridge → New scaffolding pattern. Chunk into 3–7 sub-ideas.

4. **Application** — How the concept works in practice. At least one worked example
   or concrete scenario. Show the concept doing something in the real world.

5. **Bridge** — Connect to what's coming next through foreshadowing, a new question,
   or a callback to earlier material. This creates forward momentum.

Not every section needs all five elements, and the proportions vary. A foundational
section might spend most of its space on explanation and application; an introductory
section might spend most of its space on hook and context.

## Transitions and connections

### Between sections

End one section with a thought that naturally sets up the beginning of the next.
Three effective patterns:

- **Consequence**: "Now that we understand X, we can tackle a harder question..."
- **Complication**: "This model works for simple cases, but what happens when..."
- **Zoom out, zoom in**: End by stepping back to a bigger picture, then start the
  next section by zooming into a new detail of that picture.

Avoid mechanical transitions: "Next, we'll discuss...", "Moving on to...",
"Now let's turn to..." These are reference-manual language.

### Within sections

**Callbacks** — references to earlier concepts — are the connective tissue that
transforms isolated chapters into a coherent narrative. Use them actively:
> Remember our example of X? Now imagine what happens when Y enters the picture.

**Running examples** that evolve across sections create strong coherence. The reader
follows a single developing scenario that gains complexity as their understanding
deepens. This creates the feeling of a story progressing rather than a list of topics
being checked off.

### Foreshadowing

Mentioning a concept briefly before its full treatment gives readers a sense of
recognition when it appears later. This creates curiosity and forward momentum.

George Loewenstein's Information Gap Theory (1994): curiosity arises when people feel
a gap between what they know and what they want to know. The gap must be right-sized:
too wide and people give up, too narrow and there's no curiosity. Reveal partial
answers and promise full resolution later.

## The "therefore or but" diagnostic

Trey Parker and Matt Stone articulated a structural test for narrative flow:

Each section should connect to the next with **"therefore"** or **"but"** — never
**"and then."**

- **"Therefore"** signals consequence: because we learned X, it follows that Y.
- **"But"** signals complication: X seems complete, but there's a problem.
- **"And then"** signals mere sequence: isolated information in arbitrary order.

To diagnose structural problems, write a one-sentence summary of each section and
connect them with "therefore," "but," or "and then." If most connections are "and then,"
the structure needs reorganizing. Rearrange sections so each one naturally motivates
or complicates the next.
