# Voice and Style

How to write prose that is conversational, clear, and engaging without being
condescending, bland, or stiff. Read this when editing for tone, sentence quality,
rhythm, or when content sounds either too academic or too dumbed-down.

## The non-condescension formula

The central paradox: you must simultaneously assume the reader knows nothing about
the specific topic and that they are intelligent. Julia Rohrer captures it: "The reader
is smart but has no idea what you are talking about."

### The Curse of Knowledge

Once you know something, you find it nearly impossible to imagine not knowing it.
Elizabeth Newton's 1990 Stanford experiment: people who tapped out well-known songs
predicted listeners would identify them 50% of the time. Actual success: 2.5%. Writers
are tappers — they hear the full meaning behind their words while readers see only the
words on the page.

The Curse manifests as:
- Unexplained jargon
- Skipped logical steps ("and therefore..." with three missing intermediate steps)
- Abstract language where concrete specifics are needed
- Assuming things are "too obvious to mention"

### Countermeasures

**Define every technical term at the point of first use.** Use the parenthetical
format: "the latency (the delay between a request and a response)." This supports
novices without interrupting experts. Don't hide the real names of things — include
correct jargon as a label so readers can search for more. Don't define words you won't
use again.

**Spell out every reasoning step.** If your explanation requires the reader to infer a
connection, make the connection explicit. Never expect readers to "connect the dots."

**Replace abstract claims with concrete examples immediately.** Don't say "Movie
popcorn has 20 grams of fat." Say "more fat than a bacon-and-eggs breakfast, a Big Mac
lunch, and a steak dinner combined."

### Words that signal condescension

Eliminate these entirely:

| Word/Phrase | Problem | Alternative |
|-------------|---------|-------------|
| Obviously | Implies reader should already know | (just state the fact) |
| Of course | Same as "obviously" | (just state the fact) |
| Clearly | Implies failure to see is reader's fault | (just state the fact) |
| Simply / Just / Merely | Minimizes legitimate complexity | "Here's how it works" |
| As everyone knows | Excludes reader from "everyone" | (just state the fact) |
| It goes without saying | Then don't say it | (just state the fact) |
| Don't worry | Implies reader is anxious | "This is one of the trickier parts" |
| It's easy | Dismisses reader's difficulty | "Let's walk through this" |

Replace minimizing language with **collaborative language**: "Let's walk through this"
rather than "You need to understand that." Acknowledge legitimate difficulty without
dramatizing it: "This is one of the trickier aspects" rather than "Don't worry, it's
easy."

### Why plain English isn't dumbing down

GOV.UK's content design research: "The more educated the person and the more specialist
their knowledge, the greater their preference for plain English." People with the
highest literacy levels prefer clear language because it lets them absorb information
faster. Writing simply doesn't alienate experts — it respects their time.

## Plain language rules

These operationalize comprehension for diverse audiences:

- **Lead with the main point.** Don't bury it after background or caveats.
- **Keep paragraphs focused.** One idea per paragraph.
- **Use clear headings.** Descriptive, not clever. The reader should know what they'll
  learn from the heading alone.
- **Prefer active voice.** "The capacitor stores energy" not "Energy is stored by the
  capacitor." Active voice is shorter, clearer, and assigns responsibility.
- **Use common words where possible.** "Use" not "utilize." "Start" not "commence."
  "Help" not "facilitate." But don't avoid a precise technical term just because it has
  more syllables — explain it and use it.
- **One idea per sentence.** If a sentence has two ideas joined by "and," consider
  splitting.

Health-communication research targets **6th–8th grade readability** for broad public
materials. That doesn't mean "dumb it down" — it means "remove avoidable friction."
Technical terms are fine; unnecessary complexity is not.

## Sentence rhythm and variety

The human ear demands variety. Monotonous sentence structure causes readers to
disengage even when the content is interesting.

### Concrete rules

- In any paragraph of 5+ sentences, include **at least one short sentence** (under 8
  words). Short sentences create emphasis. They punctuate.
- Avoid **more than three consecutive sentences of similar length.**
- Never begin **more than two consecutive sentences with the same word or phrase.**
- After a complex multi-clause sentence, follow with **something short and direct.**
- Limit **transition-word starters** ("However," "Additionally," "Furthermore,"
  "Moreover"). Delete any transition that doesn't change the meaning when removed.

### The Klinkenborg principle

From Verlyn Klinkenborg's *Several Short Sentences About Writing*:

- Every sentence must do useful work. No sentence can afford to be merely transitional.
- Strong long sentences are strong short sentences joined — not short sentences padded
  with qualifiers and hedges.
- Sentences that "volunteer themselves" — that come easily and feel natural — are nearly
  always clichéd. Scrutinize them.
- The simplest and most powerful revision is deletion.

### The Zinsser test

From William Zinsser's *On Writing Well*:

> "If a phrase comes to you easily, look at it with deep suspicion."

This is the single most important cliché-detection heuristic. Easy phrases are almost
always prefabricated. The good stuff requires effort.

Zinsser's core rules: strip every sentence to its cleanest components. Use active
verbs. Never say anything in writing you wouldn't say in conversation. If a sentence
can lose a word without losing meaning, lose the word.

## Clarity through sentence structure

Joseph Williams' *Style: Toward Clarity and Grace* provides two structural principles
that transform dense prose.

### Characters as subjects, actions as verbs

Dense, unclear prose often hides the real actors behind abstract nouns (nominalizations).

**Before (nominalizations):**
> The investigation of the matter by the committee resulted in a recommendation for
> the modification of the procedure.

**After (characters as subjects, actions as verbs):**
> The committee investigated the matter and recommended modifying the procedure.

**Detection method:** Scan for nominalization suffixes: **-tion, -ment, -ness, -ity,
-ence, -ance, -ing** (when used as a noun). When you find one, ask: "Who is doing
what?" Then rewrite with that person as the subject and that action as the verb.

Common nominalizations to convert:

| Nominalization | Verb form |
|----------------|-----------|
| investigation | investigate |
| recommendation | recommend |
| implementation | implement |
| utilization | use |
| establishment | establish |
| consideration | consider |
| determination | determine |
| modification | modify |
| communication | communicate |
| demonstration | demonstrate |

### The old-before-new principle

Start sentences with familiar information; end them with new information.

The end of the sentence is the **stress position** — where readers naturally place
emphasis. Putting new, important information there, and leading in with what the reader
already knows, creates a sense of flow that readers experience as "good writing" without
being able to articulate why.

**Weak (new information first):**
> A complex interaction between voltage, current, and resistance governs every circuit.

**Strong (familiar first, new last):**
> Every circuit is governed by the interaction between three things: voltage, current,
> and resistance.

The reader already knows what a circuit is (familiar); the three-way interaction is the
new information that gets the stress position.

## Eliminating clutter

Common clutter words and their replacements:

| Cluttered | Clean |
|-----------|-------|
| at the present time | now |
| in the event of | if |
| in order to | to |
| due to the fact that | because |
| it is important to note that | (delete entirely) |
| the fact that | that |
| in spite of the fact that | although |
| a large number of | many |
| in the near future | soon |
| has the ability to | can |
| on a daily basis | daily |
| for the purpose of | to / for |

Also eliminate **hedge stacking** — multiple hedges in one sentence. "It seems that
perhaps this might possibly be..." Pick one hedge or none.

## The conversational test

Read every sentence aloud (or in your head with a spoken cadence). If you wouldn't say
it that way in conversation, rewrite it.

This doesn't mean writing should be as informal as speech. It means writing should have
the directness and naturalness of speech, while having the precision and structure of
edited prose. The test eliminates most stiff, academic constructions automatically.

A corollary: if you find yourself writing something that sounds like a textbook,
imagine explaining the same thing to a friend who asked about it. Write what you'd say
to them.
